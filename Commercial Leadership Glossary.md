Terms introduced in Episode Zero

Brutal truths
Source: Willie Pietersen, business strategist and creator of the Strategic Learning framework.
Definition: An honest, unflinching assessment of current reality before any strategy is built. A brutal truth does not soften, qualify, or adjust findings to protect feelings, preserve consensus, or align with what decision-makers want to hear. It is the diagnostic prerequisite for strategy: you cannot build an accurate map from a false starting position. In commercial work, brutal truths most often surface around pricing, value capture, cross-functional alignment, and the gap between stated commercial maturity and actual commercial maturity.

Mirror imaging
Source: Frank Watanabe, CIA Directorate of Intelligence analyst, "How to Succeed in the DI," 1995.
Definition: Projecting your own thought process, logic, or value system onto another party. Assuming that a customer, competitor, or internal stakeholder sees the situation the way you do. Watanabe identified this as one of the greatest threats to objective analysis. In commercial work, mirror imaging produces pricing assumptions that do not reflect customer value perception, competitive assessments that assume rational behaviour matching your own, and internal business cases built on assumptions the organisation is actually unlikely to accept.

Commercial Maturity Model
Definition: A diagnostic framework used by IFS Group Commercial to assess commercial effectiveness across five dimensions and five levels of maturity. The five dimensions are: commercial strategy and value design, commercial execution and governance, data and decision making, capabilities and operating model, and cross-functional value realisation. The five levels run from Level One (Reactive) through Level Two (Controlled), Level Three (Efficient), Level Four (Optimised), and Level Five (Strategic and Dynamic). The model measures both team maturity, the commercial function itself, and business maturity, the wider organisation. Guiding principle: maturity is measured by the speed and certainty with which commercial value is captured, not by the sophistication of the tools.

Maturity Level One: Reactive
Definition: Commercial priorities are undocumented and shift with urgent requests. Revenue exists but leaks. Decisions are made ad hoc. There is no structured commercialisation process. The function sells what it has rather than what is commercially optimal.

Maturity Level Two: Controlled
Definition: Priorities are documented. There is a stated connection between commercial activity and revenue or earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation targets. Basic governance structures exist. Leakage is reduced and baseline profitability is established.

Maturity Level Three: Efficient
Definition: Scalable execution. Priorities are cascaded to relevant stakeholders. Each priority has a defined owner and success metric. Faster deal cycles and more consistent outcomes than Level Two.

Maturity Level Four: Optimised
Definition: Value-based commercial decisions. Priorities are linked to quantified earnings impact. Strategic trade-offs are made using data rather than politics. The commercial team operates with increasing autonomy and strategic influence.

Maturity Level Five: Strategic and Dynamic
Definition: Predictive, algorithmic, and continuous value capture. Commercial strategy is a standing agenda item at executive and board level. Priorities adjust dynamically based on market signals and performance data. The commercial function is recognised externally as a strategic differentiator.

Value realisation
Definition: The actual capture of commercial value that has already been created. Distinct from value creation, which is the design and delivery of a product or service. A commercial function can create significant value and still underperform on value realisation if pricing, positioning, governance, or cross-functional alignment are weak. Closing the realisation gap is the primary objective of commercial maturity development.

EBITDA
Definition: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation. A measure of operational profitability used throughout this series as a proxy for commercial performance at the organisational level. Spell out in full on first use per episode.

High-payoff activities
Definition: Activities that directly and measurably move commercial outcomes forward: revenue, margin, customer relationships, strategic positioning, or capability development. Distinguished from low-payoff activities, which may be necessary but do not compound commercial value. The discipline of identifying and protecting high-payoff time is the starting point of Group One.

Cross-functional value realisation
Definition: The process by which commercial value is captured through aligned action across functions that do not sit within the commercial team. Typically involves Product, Finance, Services, and Sales. A commercial function operating at Level Three or above requires that functional leaders outside the commercial team understand and own commercial outcomes, not just delegate to the commercial team. One of the five dimensions of the Commercial Maturity Model.

Spaced repetition
Definition: A learning methodology in which material is revisited at increasing intervals over time, allowing concepts to move from short-term to long-term memory. The development framework behind this series is explicitly built on this principle. Each episode is designed to be listened to more than once. Returning to material after a gap is not inefficiency. It is the design.

Artificial intelligence (AI)
Definition: Computational systems capable of performing tasks that have historically required human cognition, including analysis, pattern recognition, language generation, and decision support. Throughout this series, artificial intelligence is referenced not as a technology topic but as an environmental condition: a change in the operating context of commercial work that compresses certain advantages and amplifies others. First abbreviated as AI after first full use.

Parallel track
Definition: The Coaching and Empowerment group of this series, which runs alongside the four main groups. Designed for commercial practitioners who also lead people directly. Can be taken at any point in the series alongside any main group. Not required for individual contributors, but relevant to all.
